The Three Eras of Submission

The system of control evolved, but the goal remained the same: extract low-cost labor from a subjugated population. Each era developed a unique blend of religious justification and legal coercion to achieve this end. Below, we explore the distinct "sermon" and the harsh reality of each period.

Era 1: Chattel Slavery (c. 1850s)

The Sermon: A "Positive Good"

In the decades before the Civil War, Southern pulpits argued that slavery was not a necessary evil, but a divinely ordained "positive good." This theology portrayed slaveholders as benevolent Christian stewards caring for a supposedly inferior race.

"Slaves, obey your earthly masters with fear and trembling, with a sincere heart, as you would Christ."

- Ephesians 6:5

This verse was the cornerstone of the pro-slavery argument, presented as a timeless command. It was racially justified by twisting the "Curse of Ham" (Genesis 9:25) into a divine mandate for Black enslavement.

The Reality: Labor as Property

The economic reality was brutal. Human beings were legally defined as chattelâ€”moveable property. Their labor, bodies, and children were owned by a master. This system, enforced by violent slave codes, generated immense wealth through crops like cotton, which was framed as evidence of God's blessing on the institution.

Controlling Entity: Private Master

Legal Framework: Slave Codes

Economic System: Chattel Bondage

Era 2: Sharecropping (c. 1880s)

The Sermon: Contentment & Contract

After the Civil War, the justification shifted. Under the sacred canopy of the "Lost Cause" civil religion, sermons emphasized patience and contentment, framing poverty not as an injustice but as a spiritual trial to be endured.

"And the soldiers likewise demanded of him, saying, And what shall we do? And he said unto them, Do violence to no man, neither accuse any falsely; and be content with your wages."

- Luke 3:14

This passage was used to discourage demands for fair pay. The sanctity of contracts was also emphasized, morally binding sharecroppers to agreements designed to keep them in perpetual debt.

The Reality: Labor Controlled by Debt

Slavery was "privatized." Instead of owning bodies, landowners owned debt. Sharecroppers, forced to buy supplies on credit at exorbitant rates, were trapped in a cycle of debt peonage. Restrictive "Black Codes" and vagrancy laws criminalized unemployment, forcing freedmen into exploitative labor contracts on the same land they had worked as slaves.

Controlling Entity: Private Landowner/Merchant

Legal Framework: Black Codes, Crop-Lien Laws

Economic System: Debt Peonage

Era 3: Mass Incarceration (c. 1990s-Present)

The Sermon: Obey the State

In the late 20th century, the "tough on crime" movement, championed by the Christian Right, provided the moral justification for mass incarceration. Crime was framed as an individual moral failure, not a systemic problem, demanding a punitive, retributive response.

"Let every person be subject to the governing authorities. For there is no authority except from God, and those that exist have been instituted by God."

- Romans 13:1

The call to obey the "master" became a call to submit to the state and its carceral system. Prison ministries, while offering hope, often focus on individual sin, depoliticizing the systemic injustices that lead to incarceration.

The Reality: Labor as Punishment

Labor was "criminalized." The 13th Amendment's exception clauseâ€”"except as a punishment for crime"â€”created a legal loophole for a new form of servitude. Mass incarceration, fueled by the War on Drugs and harsh sentencing, created a vast, cheap labor pool for states and private corporations, with incarcerated workers earning pennies an hour. This is the modern heir to the convict leasing system.

Controlling Entity: State & Private Corporations

Legal Framework: 13th Amendment Exception Clause

Economic System: Carceral Labor

The Data of Disparity

The economic systems of control were never race-neutral. At every stage, they were designed to disproportionately target and control Black labor. The data clearly reveals the foundational economics and the racialized nature of these systems.

Slavery: The Nation's Asset (1860)

The stolen value of enslaved people was the single largest financial asset in the U.S. economy, worth more than all manufacturing and railroads combined.

Source: "Slavery's Capitalism" by Sven Beckert & Seth Rockman

Sharecropping: A Racial Trap (MS, 1900)

While many poor whites were sharecroppers, the system trapped a far higher percentage of the Black farming population, preventing economic independence.

Source: U.S. Census Data, 1900

Incarceration: A Racial Disparity (NC, 2022)

The legacy of racial control is starkly visible in modern prison demographics, where Black citizens are dramatically overrepresented.

Source: N.C. Dept. of Public Safety

The Counter-Pulpit of Resistance

The sermons of submission never went unchallenged. In every era, a powerful counter-theology of liberation existed, championed by abolitionists, the Black Church, and justice advocates who read the same Bible and found a message of freedom, not bondage.

Theology of Liberation

Abolitionists like Frederick Douglass and enslaved Christians drew hope from passages about equality and freedom. The story of the Exodusâ€”God liberating the oppressed from bondageâ€”provided a revolutionary motivation that directly contradicted the slaveholder's theology.

"There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free, nor is there male and female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus."

- Galatians 3:28

The Black Church & Modern Dissent

After emancipation, the Black Church became the epicenter of resistance, organizing for political and economic power. Today, Black faith leaders and a growing number of conservative and mainline Christians are at the forefront of the movement to end mass incarceration, arguing for a justice that is restorative, not just retributive.

This dissent shows that the theological consensus that has propped up these systems is fracturing.
