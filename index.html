The Three Eras of Submission

The system of control evolved, but the goal remained the same: extract low-cost labor from a subjugated population. Each era developed a unique blend of religious justification and legal coercion.

Slavery (c. 1850s)

The Sermon: A "Positive Good"

Pulpits argued slavery was divinely ordained, portraying masters as benevolent stewards.

"Slaves, obey your earthly masters with fear and trembling..." - Eph. 6:5

The Reality: Labor as Property

Humans were legally defined as chattel. Slave codes enforced this system, generating immense wealth through forced labor.

Sharecropping (c. 1880s)

The Sermon: Contentment

The "Lost Cause" civil religion framed poverty as a spiritual trial and emphasized the sanctity of contracts.

"Be content with your wages." - Luke 3:14

The Reality: Labor Controlled by Debt

Slavery was privatized into debt peonage, enforced by Black Codes and exploitative crop-lien laws.

Incarceration (c. 1990s)

The Sermon: Obey the State

The "tough on crime" movement framed crime as individual moral failure demanding a punitive response.

"Be subject to the governing authorities." - Romans 13:1

The Reality: Labor as Punishment

The 13th Amendment's loophole was used to create a vast, cheap labor pool via mass incarceration.

The Data of Disparity

At every stage, these systems were designed to disproportionately target and control Black labor. The data reveals the foundational economics and the resulting racialized disparities.

Slavery: The Nation's Primary Asset

In 1860, the financial value of enslaved people was the single largest asset in the US, worth more than all manufacturing and railroads combined.

Source: "Slavery's Capitalism", Beckert & Rockman

Sharecropping: A Racial Trap

By 1900 in Mississippi, the sharecropping system had trapped a far higher percentage of the Black farming population than the white.

Source: U.S. Census Data, 1900

Incarceration: A Stark Disparity

In North Carolina today (2022), Black people are dramatically overrepresented in prisons compared to their share of the general population.

Source: N.C. Dept. of Public Safety, 2022

Resistance & A Call for Reckoning

The sermons of submission never went unchallenged. This history demands critical thought and action today.

The Counter-Pulpit

In every era, a powerful counter-theology of liberation existed. Abolitionists and the Black Church drew on passages of equality and the Exodus story—God liberating the oppressed—to fight for freedom.

"There is neither Jew nor Gentile, neither slave nor free... for you are all one in Christ Jesus." - Galatians 3:28
